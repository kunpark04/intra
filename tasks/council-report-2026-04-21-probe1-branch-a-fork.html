Blind Spots the Council Caught (Peer Review)

What ALL five advisors missed

  • Economic floor. No advisor — not even the First Principles Thinker at the headline level — priced what a successful Option Y actually pays. On $50k starting equity at flat $500 risk, Sharpe 1.5 ≈ $3–5k/year gross. After friction and taxes, this is minimum wage on research hours. A preregistration gate that only measures Sharpe but not net dollars can pass while still being an economically unjustifiable use of time.
  • Gate calibration. N_1.3 ≥ 10 was chosen by convention, not power analysis. The 9-vs-10 outcome sits inside noise of the gate itself. Neither "passed by 1" nor "missed by 1" carries strong posterior weight.
  • Combo 865 is a 1-day test, not a 5-day sweep. The full Option Y over-spends to answer a question the Expansionist's own reasoning can bind cheaply: if 865 isn't robust on fresh data, Y is dead before session axes matter; if 865 is robust, session-axis attribution is worth paying for.
  • Opportunity cost is in hobbyist hours, not compute-dollars. Five days of remote compute is ~$5. Five days of the user's attention is the scarce resource no advisor priced.

Individual blind spots caught

  • Outsider (3/5 reviewers flagged): Internal contradiction — "fix the V3/V4 auditor first" and "run Option Y next" cannot both be the next step. Chairman discounts the Outsider's Y vote proportionally; effective tally is closer to 2-Y / 2-Z / 1-abstain than the headline 3-2.

Chairman's Logic

Why the majority isn't sufficient:

  • Headline vote is 3-Y / 2-Z, but the Outsider's Y vote is internally contradicted and one of the 3 is de facto abstaining. That puts the real signal at 2-Y / 2-Z with one blind-spotted vote — not a clear win for Y.

  • The Expansionist's "combo 865 is the first real signal" claim is testable for one day of compute. If the Expansionist is right, the 5-day Y sweep is worth funding after the cheap test confirms the premise.

The hybrid recommendation is the only path that (a) honors the peer-reviewer consensus on the economic floor, (b) doesn't abandon the Expansionist's positive observation, and (c) respects the Contrarian's and First-Principles Thinker's time-budget argument.
